## Detailed Template Breakdown

### A) Reading Comprehension (6 templates)
**Purpose:** Extract information, infer meaning, evaluate arguments, draw conclusions

1. **VER_RC_L1_001** – Direct factual question (stated explicitly in passage)
2. **VER_RC_L1_002** – Identify stated relationship (two ideas connected in text)
3. **VER_RC_L2_003** – Infer meaning from passage (implicit, needs one inference step)
4. **VER_RC_L2_004** – Identify author intent or tone (what is author trying to convey?)
5. **VER_RC_L3_005** – Evaluate argument within passage (is author's reasoning sound?)
6. **VER_RC_L3_006** – Decide what must/cannot be concluded (strict logical inference)

**Parameters:**
- Passage length: L1 (60–120 words), L2 (120–200 words), L3 (200–350 words)
- Sentence structure: Simple, Complex, Dense
- Question type: Factual, Inferential, Evaluative
- Distractor logic: Paraphrases, reversals, partially supported claims

**Example (TCS Easy):** 
Passage: "The new policy encourages remote work. Many employees prefer working from home."
Q: "What does the passage suggest about employee preferences?"
A: "Employees prefer remote work" (directly supported)

**Example (GATE Hard):**
Passage: "The study found students using AI tools scored 15% higher. However, the control group had weaker initial background. The researchers claim AI is effective, but confounding variables exist..."
Q: "What assumption is necessary for the conclusion?"
A: Requires synthesizing multiple ideas, identifying hidden assumptions

---

### B) Critical Reasoning (Arguments) (6 templates)
**Purpose:** Identify components of arguments, evaluate strength, strengthen/weaken claims

7. **VER_CR_L1_007** – Identify conclusion (which statement is the main claim?)
8. **VER_CR_L1_008** – Identify premise (which fact supports the conclusion?)
9. **VER_CR_L2_009** – Identify assumption (what must be true for this argument to work?)
10. **VER_CR_L2_010** – Strengthen or weaken argument (choose statement that helps/hurts)
11. **VER_CR_L3_011** – Evaluate argument validity (is the reasoning logically sound?)
12. **VER_CR_L3_012** – Choose statement that must be true (strict deduction from argument)

**Parameters:**
- Argument structure: Simple (1 premise→1 conclusion), Complex (3+ premises)
- Argument type: Deductive, Inductive, Causal, Analogical
- Fallacy types (for L3): Ad hominem, begging the question, false dichotomy
- Option types: Strengthen, weaken, resolve, rebut

**Example (TCS Easy):**
Argument: "All employees are trained. John is an employee. Therefore, John is trained."
Q: "What is the conclusion?"
A: "John is trained"

**Example (GATE Hard):**
Argument: "Company X launched a new product. Sales increased 20% the next quarter. Therefore, the product caused the sales increase."
Q: "Which statement would most weaken this conclusion?"
A: "Competitor Y's sales also increased 18% without launching a new product" (establishes alternative explanation)

### C) Statement–Assumption–Conclusion (5 templates)
**Purpose:** Identify assumptions underlying statements, draw valid conclusions

13. **VER_SAC_L1_013** – Statement vs conclusion (is this a valid conclusion?)
14. **VER_SAC_L1_014** – Statement vs assumption (does the speaker assume this?)
15. **VER_SAC_L2_015** – Multiple assumptions (which assumptions support this claim?)
16. **VER_SAC_L2_016** – Multiple conclusions (which conclusions logically follow?)
17. **VER_SAC_L3_017** – Determine logically valid inferences (strict logical validity)

**Parameters:**
- Statement types: Declarative, Prescriptive, Evaluative
- Assumption complexity: Explicit, Implicit, Hidden
- Conclusion types: Necessary, Probable, Possible
- Inference gap: 1 step (L1), 2 steps (L2), 3+ steps (L3)

**Example (TCS Easy):**
Statement: "Only hardworking students pass."
Assumption: "Lazy students do not pass"
Q: "Does the speaker assume this?"
A: Yes (logical consequence)

**Example (GATE Hard):**
Statement: "The government increased education spending, yet literacy rates remain unchanged."
Q: "Which assumption would make this paradox disappear?"
A: "Funds were diverted before reaching schools" (resolves apparent contradiction)

---

### D) Cause–Effect & Inference (5 templates)
**Purpose:** Identify causal relationships, distinguish correlation from causation, evaluate claims

18. **VER_CE_L1_018** – Direct cause–effect (A happened, then B. A caused B?)
19. **VER_CE_L2_019** – Implied cause or effect (not explicitly stated)
20. **VER_CE_L2_020** – Distinguish correlation vs causation (both present, which is which?)
21. **VER_CE_L3_021** – Evaluate causal claim (is the evidence sufficient?)
22. **VER_CE_L3_022** – Select valid inference (what can we reliably conclude?)

**Parameters:**
- Causal types: Direct, Indirect, Necessary, Sufficient, Coincidental
- Evidence strength: Strong, Moderate, Weak
- Alternative explanations: Present, Absent
- Temporal relationships: Clear, Ambiguous

**Example (TCS Easy):**
"The temperature dropped 5°C. The ice melted. Q: Did the temperature drop cause the melting?"
A: No (temperature drop would freeze, not melt)

**Example (GATE Hard):**
"Company A reduced prices. Customer satisfaction increased. Q: Did price reduction cause satisfaction increase?"
A: Requires evaluating alternative causes (better service, product quality improvement, market perception)

---

### E) Sentence Completion (Reasoning-based) (4 templates)
**Purpose:** Complete sentences using logic, identify consistency, evaluate options

23. **VER_SC_L1_023** – Complete sentence using logic (simple contextual fit)
24. **VER_SC_L2_024** – Choose option consistent with context (reasoning required)
25. **VER_SC_L2_025** – Eliminate logically inconsistent options (find contradiction)
26. **VER_SC_L3_026** – Evaluate best logical completion (most coherent, nuanced choice)

**Parameters:**
- Sentence structure: Simple, Complex, Compound
- Reasoning type: Parallel logic, Contrast, Causal, Conditional
- Distractor types: Synonyms, Opposites, Contextually wrong
- Coherence level: Surface (L1), Semantic (L2), Pragmatic (L3)

**Example (TCS Easy):**
"Although the plan was innovative, it was ___ due to budget constraints."
A: Rejected (opposite of expected, fits 'Although')

**Example (GATE Hard):**
"The research, which initially seemed promising, ultimately revealed that ___."
A: Requires understanding narrative structure and stakes (not just any negative outcome)

---

### F) Para-jumbles / Logical Ordering (4 templates)
**Purpose:** Reorder sentences to form coherent argument, identify logical flow

27. **VER_PJ_L1_027** – Simple sentence ordering (clear sequence, no ambiguity)
28. **VER_PJ_L2_028** – Ordering with connectors (use linking words to order)
29. **VER_PJ_L2_029** – Paragraph coherence (maintain topic + logical flow)
30. **VER_PJ_L3_030** – Choose ordering preserving argument flow (complex argument structure)

**Parameters:**
- Sentence count: 4–5 sentences
- Argument structure: Linear (simple sequence), Branching (sub-points), Nested
- Connector types: Temporal (then, subsequently), Causal (therefore, because), Contrastive (however)
- Paragraph types: Explanatory, Argumentative, Narrative

**Example (TCS Easy):**
Sentences: 
A) First, heat the oil.
B) Then, add the spices.
C) Finally, add vegetables.
Order: A → B → C (obvious temporal sequence)

**Example (GATE Hard):**
Sentences (no explicit connectors):
A) The proposal has merit.
B) Critics raise valid concerns.
C) However, implementation faces political obstacles.
D) Thus, success depends on stakeholder buy-in.
Order: A → B → D → C? or A → B → C → D? (Requires understanding logical argument structure)

### G) Analogies (Conceptual, not vocabulary) (4 templates)
**Purpose:** Identify conceptual relationships, avoid vocabulary-only analogies

31. **VER_AN_L1_031** – Simple conceptual analogy (A:B :: C:D, one relationship)
32. **VER_AN_L2_032** – Multi-relationship analogy (multiple dimensions must align)
33. **VER_AN_L2_033** – Eliminate incorrect analogy (identify broken relationship)
34. **VER_AN_L3_034** – Evaluate analogy consistency (is analogy logically sound?)

**Parameters:**
- Relationship types: Functional (tool:function), Structural (part:whole), Causal, Hierarchical
- Complexity: Simple pairing (L1), Multi-attribute (L2), Nuanced (L3)
- Distractor strategy: Tempting but wrong relationships
- Domain: Universal concepts, not specialized knowledge

**Example (TCS Easy):**
"Pen is to writing as ___ is to painting."
A: Brush (same functional relationship)

**Example (GATE Hard):**
"Skepticism is to belief as catalyst is to ___."
A: Requires understanding that skepticism accelerates questioning (like catalyst speeds reaction), not blocks belief
A: "Reaction" (analog: catalyst speeds reaction, skepticism speeds inquiry, not negates)

### H) Classification & Odd-One-Out (Conceptual) (4 templates)
**Purpose:** Classify by abstract properties, identify odd-one-out by reasoning

35. **VER_CL_L1_035** – Identify odd-one-out (obvious single distinguishing attribute)
36. **VER_CL_L2_036** – Classification by abstract property (not surface features)
37. **VER_CL_L2_037** – Eliminate distractors by reasoning (multiple properties, pick rule)
38. **VER_CL_L3_038** – Justify classification rule (explain why grouping is valid)

**Parameters:**
- Classification basis: Categorical (semantic field), Functional, Hierarchical, Abstract
- Property count: Single (L1), Multiple (L2), Competing/conflicting (L3)
- Item complexity: Concrete words, Abstract concepts, Nuanced terms
- Distractor: Partially matching, superficially similar, contextually related

**Example (TCS Easy):**
"Find odd-one-out: A) Lion, B) Tiger, C) Elephant, D) Zebra"
A: Elephant (all others are carnivores; elephant is herbivore)

**Example (GATE Hard):**
"Find odd-one-out: A) Pragmatism, B) Skepticism, C) Idealism, D) Realism"
A: Requires understanding philosophical stances. Pragmatism (judges by utility), but others are epistemological (C,D) or methodological (B). Which is odd?
A: Likely Skepticism (questions knowledge itself, while others position epistemic standpoint)

## Parameter Envelopes (Structural, Text-Based)

### Non-Engineering (TCS Easy)
- **Passage length:** 60–150 words (L1), 120–180 words (L2)
- **Sentence structure:** Simple (avg 12–15 words), occasional complex
- **Vocabulary:** Common, no specialized terminology
- **Topic:** Everyday, relatable (school, workplace, general knowledge)
- **Reasoning gap:** 0–1 inference steps (stated or immediate)
- **Ambiguity:** Minimal (1 clearly correct answer)
- **Cultural context:** Neutral, no regional references

**Example:** "Remote work policies allow flexibility. Many prefer home offices. Question: Do employees prefer remote work?" → Answer directly supported

### Engineering (GATE/GMAT Hard)
- **Passage length:** 200–350 words (L3), dense paragraphs
- **Sentence structure:** Complex (avg 18–25 words), varied subordination
- **Vocabulary:** Academic, technical terms allowed
- **Topic:** Abstract concepts (business, science, policy, philosophy)
- **Reasoning gap:** 2–4 inference steps (requires synthesis)
- **Ambiguity:** Minimal but requires careful analysis
- **Cultural context:** Universal concepts, no advantage to specific background

**Example:** "Research suggests AI tools improve performance. However, prior ability differences could confound results. Question: What assumption would strengthen the AI claim?" → Requires identifying confounding variable and assumption about control/comparison

## Verification Logic (Verbal Ability)

### Reading Comprehension Verification
- **Passage clarity check:** Is passage unambiguous? No multiple interpretations?
- **Question-answer alignment:** Does correct answer logically follow from passage?
- **Distractor realism:** Are distractors plausible but clearly wrong?
- **Reject if:** Multiple answers supported, answer requires outside knowledge, ambiguous phrasing

### Critical Reasoning Verification
- **Argument structure:** Can conclusion be logically derived? (For deductive arguments)
- **Assumption identification:** Is stated assumption necessary and sufficient?
- **Strengthening/weakening:** Does option actually strengthen/weaken? (Not just related)
- **Reject if:** Multiple valid conclusions, necessary assumptions missing, fallacies unintended

### Inference & Assumption Verification (Claude-based)
- Use Claude to generate: "For this statement, what assumption must be true?"
- Compare with options: Does identified assumption match correct option?
- Validate distractors: Are they plausible but incorrect?
- Reject if:** Cannot reliably identify unique assumption

### Analogy & Classification Verification
- **Relationship extraction:** What is the core relationship?
- **Distractor analysis:** Does each distractor break relationship in clear way?
- **Reject if:** Multiple interpretations, relationship ambiguous, cultural bias present

### Embedding-based Deduplication
- Encode passage text as embedding (vector)
- Compare recent passages: Are any >0.85 cosine similarity?
- Reject if:** Passage too similar to recent ones (avoid topic repetition)

## Why Verbal is Different

### Verbal Reasoning ≠ Vocabulary Testing
This factory generates reasoning questions **where language is the medium**, not the message.

**What we DON'T do:**
- Synonym/antonym pairing (pure vocab)
- Grammar/spelling corrections
- Reading niche literature or cultural references
- Testing domain-specific jargon

**What we DO:**
- Comprehend implicit meaning
- Evaluate logical validity of arguments
- Identify hidden assumptions
- Distinguish causal relationships
- Classify by abstract properties
- Infer conclusions from text

## Sample Difficulty Progression

### Reading Comprehension L1 → L3
```
L1: Passage 60 words, Q: "What does author say about X?"
    Answer: Directly quoted/paraphrased in text
    
L2: Passage 150 words, Q: "Why did author mention X?"
    Answer: Requires 1 inference (linking ideas)
    
L3: Passage 300 words, Q: "Is author's conclusion warranted?"
    Answer: Requires 3+ inferences, weighing evidence
```

### Critical Reasoning L1 → L3
```
L1: Identify conclusion in simple argument
    "All X are Y. Z is X. Therefore Z is Y. → Conclusion?"
    
L2: Identify assumption in complex argument
    "Company A raised prices. Sales fell. → What's assumed?"
    (Assumes price is main factor, not service quality, etc.)
    
L3: Evaluate validity of argument with multiple premises
    "A happened, then B. B caused decline. A→B→decline true?"
    (Requires checking logic, alternative causation, evidence)
```

## Note on Generation Challenges

**Verbal is hardest to auto-generate:**
1. Claude generates passage, but ambiguity hard to detect algorithmically
2. Multiple defensible answers possible in real language
3. Embedding-based dedup not perfect (similar topics but different angles)

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Use Claude in verification mode (is answer unambiguous?)
- Higher rejection threshold for Verbal (vs Numerical/Logical)
- More aggressive manual review (start with 30%, maybe stay >10%)
- Build library of "known-good" passages and gradually rotate
